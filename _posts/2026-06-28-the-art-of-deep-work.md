Cal Newport's concept of "deep work" — the ability to focus without distraction on a cognitively demanding task — has never been more relevant, or more difficult.

## The Problem

Between Slack notifications, email pings, and the infinite scroll of social media, the modern knowledge worker is trapped in a state of perpetual shallow attention. We're busy all day but rarely productive.

## Strategies That Work

### 1. Time Blocking

Schedule deep work sessions like meetings. Put them on your calendar and treat them as non-negotiable. Two 90-minute blocks per day can accomplish more than eight hours of fragmented attention.

### 2. Ritualize the Start

Create a consistent routine that signals your brain: "We're going deep now." It could be making a cup of tea, putting on noise-canceling headphones, or closing every tab and app except the one you need.

### 3. Embrace Boredom

The inability to sit with boredom is what drives us to distraction. Practice doing nothing — standing in line without your phone, sitting with your thoughts. If you train your brain to always seek stimulation, deep work becomes impossible.

### 4. Measure Input, Not Output

Track hours of deep work, not tasks completed. The former drives the latter. Five hours of true deep work per day is world-class.

## The Payoff

Deep work isn't just about getting more done. It's about doing work that matters. The craftsman mindset — focusing on what you're producing rather than what you're getting — leads to both better work and greater satisfaction.
